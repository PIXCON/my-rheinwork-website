"Minimum viable product" is one of the most misunderstood phrases in software. Too
often it becomes an excuse for shortcuts that quietly turn into months of cleanup later.

## Small scope, solid foundation

The trick is to be ruthless about scope and careful about quality. We cut features, not
craft. A good MVP does one thing well, with a foundation that can carry the next ten
features without a rewrite.

That usually means: a clean data model, sensible boundaries between parts of the system,
and just enough automation to deploy with confidence.

## Learn fast, then build on it

The point of an MVP is to learn. So we make it easy to measure what users actually do and
quick to change course. When the early version is built properly, those learnings turn into
real momentum instead of technical debt.

Ship small. Ship solid. Then keep going.
