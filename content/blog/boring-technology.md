There is a quiet superpower in choosing technology that is, frankly, a little boring.
Proven databases, well-understood frameworks and tools with a decade of battle scars
tend to fail in predictable ways — and predictable is exactly what you want when real
users depend on your software.

## New is not the same as better

Every year brings a wave of frameworks promising to change everything. Some of them
genuinely do. But most of the value in a product comes from getting the fundamentals
right, not from the newest abstraction on the front page of a developer forum.

When we evaluate a tool, we ask three simple questions:

- Will it still be maintained in five years?
- Can the next engineer understand it quickly?
- Does it fail in ways we can reason about?

## Boring where it counts, bold where it pays off

This does not mean we never use new things. It means we spend our "innovation budget"
deliberately — on the parts of the product that actually differentiate it, and keep the
foundation calm and dependable.

That balance is what lets us move fast without waking up at 3 a.m. wondering why
production is on fire.
